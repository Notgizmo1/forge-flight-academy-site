Agencies are spending six figures on UAS platforms and five figures on operator training.

That math doesn't work, and the gap it creates between platform capability and operator proficiency is producing the same result as buying a platform that doesn't meet the mission requirement: expensive equipment that isn't being used to its potential, operated by personnel who aren't confident in their ability to deploy it effectively.

The platforms aren't the problem. The training model is.

## What "Trained" Actually Means

Part 107 certification from the FAA is the legal threshold for commercial UAS operations. It is not an operational readiness standard.

Part 107 certifies knowledge of airspace classifications, basic aeronautical regulations, weather interpretation, and sectional chart reading. Those are necessary foundations. They are not sufficient for mission employment.

A Part 107 certificate means your operator can legally fly. It doesn't mean they can execute the mission you purchased the platform for.

The gap between legal authorization and operational proficiency includes:

**Mission Planning:** Airspace coordination and authorization, contingency routing for communication loss or GPS denial, abort criteria development, weather decision frameworks beyond "go/no-go," multi-platform deconfliction for fleet operations.

**Sensor Employment:** Understanding what each payload is actually capturing and what it isn't, recognition of sensor artifact and data quality issues, real-time interpretation under operational time pressure, geo-tagging accuracy and metadata integrity.

**Platform Maintenance:** Pre-flight and post-flight inspection to standard — genuine familiarity with what failure signatures look like before they become failures. Field-level troubleshooting. Battery management that extends platform lifecycle. Motor and propulsion system inspection.

**Systems Integration:** How the UAS fits into your existing operational picture. Communication with other units during deployment. Data flow from aircraft to ground station to the personnel who need the information.

## The Parallel That Makes This Clear

A driver's license certifies you can legally operate a vehicle. It doesn't qualify you to drive an ambulance, operate a fire apparatus, or run a pursuit vehicle under emergency conditions.

Public safety agencies understand this completely when it comes to ground vehicles. Emergency vehicle operators complete significant additional training because the consequence of undertraining is visible and immediate.

UAS operations carry the same operational complexity. The consequence of undertraining is less visible — it shows up as missed data collection opportunities, platform incidents during deployment, operators who hesitate at critical moments, and platforms that get left in the case because the mission profile feels outside the operator's comfort zone.

## The Recurring Training Problem

Initial certification addresses the starting point. Proficiency requires recurring training.

Platform currency decays. New mission requirements emerge. New operators join the team. Software updates change platform behavior. Payload configurations change.

An operator who completed initial training 18 months ago and has flown minimal hours since is not at the same proficiency level as when they completed training. In aviation, currency requirements exist in the regulatory framework because proficiency without recency is not reliable proficiency. UAS programs that don't have a defined recurring training cadence are managing a proficiency problem they haven't formally acknowledged yet.

## What the Right Training Model Looks Like

Effective UAS operator training programs are platform-specific, not generic; mission-profile specific rather than built around controlled evaluation conditions; maintenance-integrated; recurring by design with a defined cadence; and assessed against operational standards — not regulatory minimums.

The agencies getting the most value from their UAS investment treated training as part of the platform acquisition — not an afterthought addressed after delivery.
