The FAA's Part 107 certification program accomplished something important: it established a regulatory framework for commercial UAS operations and created a baseline knowledge requirement for operators. Before Part 107, there was no standardized legal threshold at all.

But a legal minimum is not an operational standard, and the widespread conflation of the two is producing a specific, costly failure pattern across public safety and enterprise UAS programs.

## What Part 107 Actually Certifies

The Part 107 knowledge test assesses airspace classifications and operating requirements, flight restrictions and NOTAMs, weather interpretation and aviation weather services, loading and performance fundamentals, emergency procedures at a conceptual level, crew resource management basics, radio communication procedures, and physiological effects of altitude.

These are foundational. Every UAS operator should understand them. They are the starting point for safe, legal operations — not the endpoint for effective operational employment.

The test does not assess platform-specific knowledge. It does not assess sensor employment. It does not assess mission planning against realistic operational scenarios. It does not assess maintenance proficiency, emergency procedure execution, or the ability to operate under time pressure with incomplete information.

## The Driver's License Parallel

A standard driver's license certifies that you understand traffic law, basic vehicle operation, and road signs. It does not qualify you to operate an ambulance under emergency conditions, drive a fire apparatus to a working structure fire, or conduct a pursuit in a law enforcement vehicle.

Public safety agencies understand this intuitively for ground vehicles. Emergency vehicle operators complete significant additional training because the operational gap between basic licensing and professional employment is obvious and consequential.

The exact same gap exists in UAS operations. It is less obvious because the consequential failures are less visible: platforms that underperform because operators don't understand their sensor systems, missions that produce poor data because nobody trained the crew on payload employment, incidents that occur because emergency procedures weren't practiced to proficiency.

## The Specific Gaps That Matter

**Platform-specific proficiency.** Part 107 is platform-agnostic. An operator who passed the test on generic UAS principles is not automatically proficient on the specific aircraft your agency operates. Platform-specific training covers the flight characteristics, failure modes, emergency procedures, and maintenance requirements of your actual aircraft.

**Sensor employment.** Most public safety UAS deployments involve a sensor payload. Understanding how to fly the platform and understanding how to employ the sensor to produce actionable data are different skill sets. An operator who is proficient at the controls but untrained on the payload is producing less value than the equipment is capable of delivering.

**Mission planning.** Operational UAS deployment requires airspace coordination, contingency planning, weather decision frameworks, and integration with incident command or operational structures. Part 107 touches the knowledge foundation. Operational mission planning training builds the execution capability.

**Emergency procedures.** Part 107 introduces emergency procedure concepts. Proficiency requires practice, scenario training, and repetition. Operators who haven't practiced their emergency procedures won't execute them reliably when they're needed.

**Currency and recency.** Proficiency without recency degrades. An operator who completed initial training 18 months ago and has accumulated minimal flight hours since is not at the same proficiency level as when they finished training.

## Building the Training Program That Fills the Gap

The right training model for operational UAS programs goes beyond initial certification in several ways: mission-profile specific scenarios built around your operational use cases, maintenance integration so operators understand their platform at the component level, a recurring training cadence that maintains proficiency between deployment cycles, and assessment against operational standards — not the Part 107 knowledge test.

Part 107 opens the door. The training program determines what's on the other side of it.
