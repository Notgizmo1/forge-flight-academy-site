After two decades in Army Special Operations — including extensive use of unmanned systems in contested environments — I can tell you with confidence that the most dangerous gap in most UAS operator training programs is not flight mechanics, not mission planning, and not maintenance. It is electronic warfare literacy.

Operators who cannot diagnose why their link went down, cannot adapt their frequency plan in real time, and cannot recognize the difference between a technical failure and an active jamming event are not just ineffective — they are a liability to the mission and to themselves.

## What the Battlefield Has Taught Us

The conflict in Ukraine has produced the most data-rich real-world UAS operational environment in history. One pattern stands out clearly: the operators who survive and continue to generate effects are the ones who understand the electromagnetic environment they are operating in. Those who treat their ground control station as a black box — push buttons, fly drone, receive video — are consistently defeated by even rudimentary electronic warfare.

This is not a Ukraine-specific problem. It is an American training doctrine problem. Most UAS operator courses spend the overwhelming majority of their time on flight mechanics and mission planning. Electronic warfare awareness is either absent or treated as a brief add-on module. The result is operators who are proficient in controlled, permissive airspace and completely unprepared for denied or degraded environments.

## The Three Literacy Gaps

In my experience, most operators have three specific gaps:

**1. No mental model of what RF interference actually looks like.** Operators see video freeze or link disconnect and default to "technical issue." They check cables, restart GCS, and try again — burning mission time while the jamming source is still active. Operators with basic RF literacy recognize the symptom immediately and begin adaptive response.

**2. No ability to evaluate frequency options in real time.** Most operators use their equipment on default frequencies and have no idea how to evaluate alternative bands or modulation schemes. When the default doesn't work, they are stuck.

**3. No awareness of their own RF signature.** Experienced operators understand that every transmission is a signature. Operators without EW training broadcast continuously, on standard frequencies, and at maximum power — providing adversaries with persistent geolocation data.

## What Proper Training Looks Like

Our FFR-201 course — Tactical RF Fundamentals for UAS Operators — addresses these gaps directly. The curriculum is built on operational lessons from current conflict environments and treats EW not as a technology topic but as an operator skill. Students leave with a working mental model of the electromagnetic environment, hands-on experience adapting to jamming conditions, and the diagnostic framework to tell the difference between equipment failure and adversary action.

This is not advanced material. A five-day course is sufficient to produce operators who are meaningfully more effective in degraded environments than operators who received no EW training. The barrier is not complexity — it is that most training programs have not prioritized it.

That is a solvable problem.
